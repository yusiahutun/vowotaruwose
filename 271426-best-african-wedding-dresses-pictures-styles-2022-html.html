    African wedding dresses have captured the world's attention as the most diverse and unique garments. Traditionally, gowns represented the end of a woman's identity as an individual and becoming a dependent wife. Today, they symbolize the end of singlehood to a new commitment and partnership.

    African themed wedding dresses come in different designs, fabrics, colours, and patterns. Brides are moving away from the white gown which has long been associated with purity and virtue. Today, people worldwide are going for African inspired wedding dresses.

    You will often meet ladies (on social media) who have no ancestral roots in Africa gracing their marriage ceremonies in African wedding attires. The gowns' bold and cute patterns, colours, and prints are beautiful. Would you love to know more about these attires and Black people's wedding customs? Below are some popular African wedding dresses and traditions the brides abide by:

    1. Burundi

    Burundi's traditional female wedding attire is called Umushanana or Mushanana. It comprises a skirt, a tank top, and a sash draped over one shoulder. The clothing comes in different fabrics, from silk to Ankara.

    The current generation mixed the Mushanana dressing style with designs from other parts of the world. For instance, one can wear an Ankara dress and throw the scarf-life clothing over one shoulder to achieve the Umshanana look.

    You can also wear a simple maxi dress with a matching scarf. Women have multiple matching shawls for each dress and fold the scarves whenever they feel like it. Also, you can complete your look with some accessories or leave it as it is.

    2. Ethiopia

    In Ethiopia, the groom and his family bring presents to the bride, including the wedding dress and jewellery before the Telosh (marriage ceremony). Habesha grooms wear the Libs while the bride puts on the Kemis. Also, the woman spends several hours getting the bridal henna tattoo, braiding her hair, and picking gold accessories for her marriage ceremony.

    Towards the end of the wedding party, the couple bows to kiss the elders' knees as respect for traditions. The couple wears the Kaba (Kabanesh for women) on the second day for the post-marriage ceremony. However, it is also common for Ethiopian women to wear white wedding dresses for church weddings.

    An Ethiopian wedding is a family affair, and the festivities continue until months or a year after the wedding day. In the Shimagelay tribe, the groom sends elders from his family to the bride's parents home to ask for her hand in marriage. The Tilosh do it on the eve of the marriage ceremony.

    In Ethiopia, marriage ceremonies mainly occur after the fasting season called Abey Tsome, the long lent offering. The season usually ends around the first of May. After choosing a material/textile, the shemane makes the wedding dress design one wants.

    Dresses of these designs have different names like Agaro, Wuberest, Emebet, Miron, Abay, Nigist, Berhan, Batinya, etc. In addition, some of the names stand for Ethiopian tribes' names. Therefore, learning about these choices will help you pick the best Ethiopian Habesha wedding dress.

    Kaba (Ethiopian wedding dress) is an Amharic name for cloak. It is made of wool or cotton and has black, green or red stripes. The clothing was usually worn by high status and aristocratic women in society.

    The bride's Kemis is usually white hand-woven cotton clothing made from shemma. The experts make the gown by sewing together long strips of woven fabric. Most Ethiopian brides make their African wedding gowns because the skill is passed down to younger generations.

    Most white African wedding dresses have a touch of traditional fabrics and accessories.     Nigeria is rich in culture and traditions because it has about 371 tribes. However, the Igbo, Hausa, and Yoruba are the dominating groups. Nigerian brides have a way of bringing out the best in fashion, especially when it is their day.

    5. Uganda

    Ugandan men wear kanzus at their marriage ceremonies. In contrast, women from central and eastern Uganda wear a dress called gomesi or busuuti with large/exaggerated shoulders then tie a sash around the waist.

    Meanwhile, women from the west and north-west regions wear a dress and a long cloth called suuka around their waists and shoulders. They almost resemble the Rwandese and Burundian's imishanana.

    Modern Ugandan women prefer maxi dresses of various designs with matching veils. Most women in this country rarely wear trousers and love floor-length dresses and skirts as symbols of respect to the men and the community. However, the trend is gradually changing in urban centres.

    Conservative Ugandan women wear wrappers around their waists to prevent their linens from getting dirty and the embarrassment of silky clothes sticking to their bodies when they become sweaty.

    6. Rwanda

    Rwanda and Burundi have many things in common, including physical appearance, languages and traditions. Also, the umshanana (plural: imishanana) attire is popular among their women.

    7. South Africa

    A traditional Zulu wedding dress comprises isicholo (a special wide hat), ibhayi (a cloak or shawl worn around the shoulders), ureyisi (necklaces) and isidwaba (cow-hide skirts). The hat signifies status, while the cloak symbolizes respect to the in-laws.

    Zulu brides are using modern fabrics to make their African wedding dress styles. You won't find them wearing cow-hide skirts, but give the trendy clothing the same respect they would have given their traditional outfits.

    The traditional Xhosa bride wears an isikhakha or imibhaco (an elegant textile skirt), iduku (a blanket or shawl around the shoulders), iqhiya (the headpiece), and uxakatha (a towel or thick scarf around her waist). The imibhaco represents a nurturing/ hardworking woman. Iqhiya means she is married, while the uxakatha protects her fertility.

    Meanwhile, today's women wear different modern African wedding dresses from above the knee-length skirts to unique colours like red and yellow. The community has also had many other attires for various stages of life. Each garment honours and preserves the dignity and beauty of Xhosa culture and ritual.

    A makoti (newlywed Xhosa bride) wears a black turban-like scarf around her head, and it is dipped low to veil her eyes as respect to the elders and in-laws. The woman would move it up and off her eyes after having her first child and wear any colour headscarf.
