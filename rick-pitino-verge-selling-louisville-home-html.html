    Rick Pitino on verge of selling Louisville home

    Suspended college basketball coach Rick Pitino is on the verge of selling his 5,100-square-foot Louisville home, it was reported on Saturday. Pitino is reportedly in contract to sell the home to a buyer.

    15 Sep 2024, 00:00

    Suspended college basketball coach Rick Pitino is on the verge of selling his 5,100-square-foot Louisville home, it was reported on Saturday.

    Pitino is reportedly in contract to sell the home to a buyer. 

    The University of Louisville head coach’s asking price is not known, though the home is expected to fetch at least $1.03million.

    The figure is based on a four-year-old assessment of the home which was done for tax purposes, according to the Courier-Journal.

    The home is located on Mockingbird Gardens Drive, which is a street in one of Louisville’s most exclusive residential areas.

    In late September, Pitino was put on unpaid administrative leave by the university after the coach was linked to a federal fraud and corruption investigation.

    The FBI alleges that Louisville coaches and an Adidas executive paid $100,000 to the father of a star recruit in exchange for his commitment to attend the Kentucky school.

    The two-story home, which was built in 2013 on an acre of land, has four bedrooms, five bathrooms, one half-bath, and an in-ground swimming pool as well as a renovated basement.

    An aerial photograph of the home taken by WAVE-TV shows a three-car garage as well.

    According to records, Pitino transferred ownership of the home to his daughter, Jacqueline, who bought it for $101,000.

    Pitino also owns homes in Indian Creek, Florida, and Bal Harbour, Florida.

    Those homes have assessed values of nearly $9 million and $3.4 million, respectively, according to the Lexington Herald Leader.

    Pitino put his home on the market just days after it was learned that he is being questioned as part of an FBI investigation into alleged bribery of college basketball players. He has denied any wrongdoing

    Pitino is expected to be informed on Monday by the university’s board of trustees that he will be dismissed from his post.

    Pitino - one of the highest paid basketball coaches in the world with an annual salary of $7.7 million - had not been linked directly to the FBI investigation.

    However, some of the most explosive allegations in the court documents appear to involve Louisville, which was already on NCAA probation over a sex scandal.

    The star recruit who allegedly received a bribe to come to Louisville has been identified as Brian Bowen.

    Bowen was also expected to eventually sign a sneaker contract with Adidas and hire certain advisers if and when he turned professional, according to the Courier-Journal.

    He is an All-American from Indiana who chose Louisville over other national powerhouse programs such as Michigan State, Arizona, UCLA, and Oregon.

    Pitino, who has denied any wrongdoing, had already been suspended for the first five games of the season after it was revealed that strippers were provided to basketball recruits by basketball operations director Andre McGee.

    Louisville is appealing the decision, but the school may ultimately be forced to vacate dozens of wins and the team's 2013 national championship if the NCAA's ruling is not overturned.

    One scheme involved an Adidas rep, James Gatto, and his associates funneling cash to top recruits in order to push them towards colleges that had sponsorship deals with Adidas

    Another scheme involved managers and advisors bribing college coaches, who persuaded top recruits and their families to sign with those managers and advisors

    (From left to right) Lamont Evans of Oklahoma State, Emanuel Richardson of Arizona, Tony Bland of USC, and Auburn's Chuck Person were all charged following an FBI investigation 

    An Adidas executive and four assistant coaches are among 10 college basketball figures charged with using hundreds of thousands of dollars in bribes to influence star athletes’ choice of shoe sponsors, agents, and schools.

    So far, no current or former officials from Louisville have been named in the complaint.

    Pitino said he agreed with prosecutors that third-party schemes initiated by a few bad actors 'operated to commit a fraud' on universities and their basketball programs.

    He said fans and supporters deserve better.

    The Louisville program has already lost a pair of top recruits to the scandal. Anfernee Simons, a 6-3, 165-pound guard from Bradenton, Fla., announced that he has rescinded his commitment to Louisville.

    Shortly thereafter, St. Louis-area guard Courtney Ramey changed his mind about the Cardinals.
