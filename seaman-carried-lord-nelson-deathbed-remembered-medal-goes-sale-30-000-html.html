    Seaman who carried Lord Nelson to his deathbed remembered as medal goes up for sale for 30,000

    The service medal awarded to one of the men who carried Admiral Lord Nelson to his deathbed andlater inspired Charles Dickens, is going up for sale for 30,000. Seaman James Sharman rushed to the side of the naval hero after he was shot by a French sniper at the Battle of Trafalgar in 1805.

    14 Jul 2024, 00:00

    The service medal awarded to one of the men who carried Admiral Lord Nelson to his deathbed and later inspired Charles Dickens, is going up for sale for £30,000.

    Seaman James Sharman rushed to the side of the naval hero after he was shot by a French sniper at the Battle of Trafalgar in 1805.

    He helped lift and carry a fatally injured Nelson down to the Orlop deck on HMS Victory where he was made comfortable.

    In his dying hours Nelson told his captain Thomas Hardy to make sure his mistress Lady Emma Hamilton and their daughter Horatia would be taken care of.

    Seaman James Sharman (pictured left, in old age) rushed to the side of the naval hero (right) after he was shot by a French sniper at the Battle of Trafalgar in 1805

    A picture showing Nelson after he was struck down at the height of the battle on the deck of HMS Victory

    He held on for long enough to hear that his men had triumphed over the combined French and Spanish fleet at Trafalgar.

    In later life, Sharman was appointed the first caretaker of the (Great) Yarmouth Column, a 144ft high monument which was erected in honour of Nelson in the Norfolk town.

    He was also the inspiration for the Charles Dickens character Ham Peggotty in his 1850 novel David Copperfield.

    Sharman's Naval Service Medal, with a Trafalgar clasp, is now being sold by a private collector with auction house Peter Wilson, of Nantwich, Cheshire.

    Sharman, from Yarmouth, worked in his youth in the 'Wrestlers Inn' which Nelson frequented at the turn of the 19th century.

    The service medal awarded to James Sharman, who carried a mortally wound Lord Nelson down from the deck where he was shot

    He started serving on board the frigate Weasel in 1803 before transferring to the Victory.

    Sharman was wounded during the battle, but he survived.

    He returned home of Yarmouth where he made headlines two decades later for an act of great bravery.

    Sharman sprung into action to rescue a crew member of the brig Hammond that was wrecked on the beach near his cottage.

    He waded into the surf, being knocked of his feet several times, and retrieved a man clinging to the wreckage in the nick of time.

    Six crew members had already been washed away to their deaths.

    A gripping newspaper account of his actions read: 'Sharman waded through the surf to where the wreck had drifted. The surf carried him three times off his legs, and the fourth time threw him with his back on the wreck.

    'After some search he discovered a man clinging to the breast-hook, who informed him the other men had been washed to sea.

    'Sharman took the rope from his own body, put it around that of the other man and they plunged together into the surf, at the same time calling to Smith (his fellow rescuer) to pull them back to the shore.

    'His actions saved the man from enduring the same fate as his six companions.'

    Pictured: HMS Victory. Sharman started serving on board the frigate Weasel in 1803 before transferring to the Victory

    Dickens read the newspaper report and arranged to visit Sharman, with the encounter taking place in the Royal Hotel.

    After their meeting, he is said to have based tragic hero Peggotty - an imposing fisherman and boatbuilder - on him.

    In the book, Peggotty drowns trying to rescue James Steerforth, a local student, during a storm at sea off Yarmouth.

    Sharman died aged 82 in 1867.

    The same vendor is also selling the medals awarded to Thomas Atkinson, who was on board the Victory at the Battle of Trafalgar and was regarded by Nelson as 'one of the best masters I have seen in the Royal Navy'.

    His medals, including the Nile medal, are expected to fetch £15,000.

    Chris Large, auctioneer at Peter Wilson, said: 'Sharman served aboard the Victory, Nelson's flagship at the Battle of Trafalgar, where recent research has confirmed that he was one of the men who carried Nelson down to the Orlop deck where he died.

    'This brought him to the notice of Captain Hardy who later was instrumental in procuring for Sharman the post of caretaker of the Yarmouth Column.

    'It was in Yarmouth that Sharman met Charles Dickens, who was staying at the Royal Hotel at the time.

    'Dickens was fascinated by Sharman, and is said to have based the character of Peggotty on him in his book David Copperfield.

    'Any medals with an association of Nelson have that magical goldust all over them and the fact these men were so close to him make them even more special.

    'Without the Sharman connection, his medal would be worth a few hundred pounds, not £20,000 to £30,000.

    'Thomas Atkinson was another Nelson man. Nelson described Atkinson as 'one of the best masters I have seen in the Royal Navy' and Atkinson can be seen in the painting 'Death of Nelson' in the Walker Art Gallery in Liverpool.

    'These are very exciting medals to sell.'

    The Nelson's Monument at Great Yarmouth shows Britannia proudly sitting atop a globe inscribed with the motto from Nelson's coat of arms.

    She holds an olive branch in her outstretched right hand, a trident in her left and looks inland towards Nelson's birthplace in Burnham Thorpe.

    The sale takes place on January 30.

    Battle of Trafalgar: Epic sea clash that laid foundations for Britain's global power - and claimed the life of Lord Admiral Nelson

     Nelson's (above) triumph at Trafalgar gave Britain control of the seas and laid the foundation for Britain's global power for more than a century

    Fought on October 21, 1805, the Battle of Trafalgar is one of history's most epic sea clashes.

    Not only did it see Britain eliminate the most serious threat to security in 200 years, but it also saw the death of British naval hero Admiral Lord Nelson.

    This was not before his high-risk, but acutely brave strategy won arguably the most decisive victory in the Napoleonic wars. Nelson's triumph gave Britain control of the seas and laid the foundation for Britain's global power for more than a century.

    Despite signing a peace treaty in 1803, the two nations were at war and fought each other in seas around the world.

    After Spain allied with France in 1804, the newly-crowned French emperor Napoleon Bonaparte had enough ships to challenge Britain.

    In October 1805, French Admiral Pierre-Charles Villeneuve led a Combined French and Spanish fleet of 33 ships from the Spanish port of Cadiz to face Nelson and Vice Admiral Cuthbert Collingwood.

    Fought on October 21, 1805, the Battle of Trafalgar is one of history's most epic sea clashes. Not only did it see Britain eliminate the most serious threat to security in 200 years, but it also saw the death of British naval hero Admiral Lord Nelson

    Nelson, fresh from chasing Villeneuve in the Caribbean, led the 27-ship fleet charge in HMS Victory, while Vice Admiral Collingwood sailed in Royal Sovereign.

    Battles at sea had until then been mainly inconclusive, as to fire upon the opposing ship, each vessel had to pull up along side one another (broadside) which often resulted in equal damage.

    Nelson bucked this trend by attacking the Combined Fleet line head on - and sailed perpendicular towards the fleet, exposing the British to heavy fire. 

    He attacked in two columns to split the Combined Fleet's line to target the flagship of Admiral Villneuve.

    11. 30am Lord Nelson famously declared that 'England expects that every man will do his duty', in reference to the command that the ships were instructed to think for themselves. The captains had been briefed on the battle plan three weeks before, and were trusted to bravely act on their own initiative and adapt to changing circumstances - unlike their opponents who stuck to their command.

    Vice Admiral Cuthbert Collingwood led the first column and attacked the rear of the line, and broke through. 

    Nelson sailed directly for the head of the Combined Fleet to dissuade them from doubling back to defend the rear. But before he reached them, he changed course to attack the middle of the line - and Villeneuve's flagship.

    Speeding toward the centre of the line, HMS Victory found no space to break through as Villeneuve's flagship was being tightly followed - forcing Nelson to ram through at close quarters. 

    In the heat of battle, and surrounded on three sides, Nelson was fatally shot in the chest by a well-drilled French musketeer.

    The Combined Fleet's vanguard finally began to come to the aid of Admiral Villeneuve, but British ships launch a counter-attack.

    Admiral Villeneuve struck his colours along with many other ships in the Combined Fleet and surrendered.

    4.14pm HMS Victory Captain Thomas Masterman Hardy dropped below deck to congratulate Nelson on his victory.

    4.30pm With the knowledge he has secured victory, but before the battle had officially concluded, Lord Nelson died. 

    5.30pm French ship Achille blew up signalling the end of the battle - in all 17 Combined Fleet ships surrendered.

     

     ... so did Nelson really say 'Kiss me, Hardy' with his dying words?

    By RICHARD CREASY for the Daily Mail (in an article from 2007) 

    It was Britain's greatest naval victory and for more than 200 years historians have analysed every detail.

    Now, amazingly, a new eye-witness account of the Battle of Trafalgar has emerged during a house clear-out.

    It gives not only a first-hand view of proceedings from the lower decks but also a different interpretation of one of history's most enduring arguments - Admiral Lord Nelson's dying words.

    Robert Hilton was a 21-year-old surgeon's mate on HMS Swiftsure, a 74-gun ship that played its part in the destruction of the French and Spanish fleets and of Napoleon's dream of invading England.

    It was 13 days later, after Swiftsure had made it through gales to Gibraltar for repairs that Hilton took up his pen and wrote a nine-page letter home on November 3, 1805.

    In it he says Nelson's last words, relayed to his ship's company from Nelson's flag captain, Captain Hardy, were: 'I have then lived long enough.'

    Many people believe Nelson said: 'Kiss me Hardy.' 

    But historians rely on his surgeon's reports that he said: 'Thank God I have done my duty.'
